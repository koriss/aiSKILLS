# Methodology
This document expands the runtime contract from `SKILL.md` into a detailed analytical method.
## 1. Question framing
Every investigation starts by converting the user request into a tractable analytical target.
Define:
- the main question;
- the practical decision or judgment the answer should support;
- the time horizon;
- the domains involved;
- the likely evidence classes;
- the likely ambiguity points.
Good framing questions:
- What exactly must be established?
- What would count as disconfirming evidence?
- Is the user asking for facts, causality, forecast, exposure map, or recommendation?
- What level of confidence is realistically possible?
Bad framing patterns:
- answering a broader question than the user asked;
- collapsing descriptive and causal questions into one;
- letting an emotionally vivid narrative define the scope.
## 2. Task decomposition
Break the task into sub-questions.
Typical sub-question types:
- event reconstruction;
- actor identification;
- motive / incentive analysis;
- capability analysis;
- timeline reconstruction;
- data verification;
- legal or regulatory context;
- scenario or forecast implications.
Each sub-question should have:
- a status,
- an evidence target,
- a current confidence level,
- a note on what would close it.
## 3. Evidence classes
Do not think in terms of “sites”. Think in terms of evidence classes.
Examples:
- official documents;
- law or regulatory text;
- scientific paper and supplementary appendix;
- company filing;
- customs or procurement record;
- speech, transcript, or direct quote;
- dataset;
- map or geospatial evidence;
- image or video with provenance;
- reputable secondary synthesis;
- contextual commentary.
This prevents the lazy pattern of reading five derivative articles that all depend on the same upstream claim.
## 4. Claim decomposition
Strong analysis is claim-driven.
Take each important narrative and turn it into atomic claims.
Examples:
Narrative:
“Company X is secretly aligned with policy Y.”
Atomic claims:
- company X funded organization Z;
- organization Z lobbied for policy Y;
- timing overlaps materially;
- executives or advisers overlap;
- money flow exists and is documented;
- there is evidence of direct coordination, or there is not.
For each claim:
- state the source;
- state whether it is direct evidence or inference;
- identify the strongest counterpoint;
- identify what would falsify it.
## 5. Timelines
A timeline is not decoration. It is a test of narrative integrity.
Build a dated sequence of:
- events,
- decisions,
- public statements,
- filings,
- changes in ownership,
- technical releases,
- legal actions,
- market reactions,
- epidemiological or operational milestones where relevant.
Use timeline analysis to detect:
- post hoc fallacies,
- selective chronology,
- suspicious simultaneity,
- retrospective narrative smoothing.
## 6. Actor analysis
For each relevant actor, identify:
- role,
- incentives,
- capabilities,
- dependencies,
- constraints,
- likely red lines,
- what they gain if a given hypothesis is true,
- what they lose.
This is especially useful in geopolitics, finance, public policy, and corporate investigations.
## 7. Hypothesis generation
Never settle for only one explanation.
Default pattern:
- H1 obvious explanation,
- H2 strongest alternative,
- H3 structural or mixed explanation.
For each hypothesis ask:
- What does it explain well?
- What does it fail to explain?
- Which assumptions are heavy?
- Which observations should exist if it is true?
- Which sources would be most likely to contradict it?
## 8. Stress testing
Before synthesis, attack your own draft.
Check for:
- unsupported causal language;
- single-source dependency;
- repetition mistaken for corroboration;
- out-of-context quote use;
- unsound extrapolation from small samples;
- pattern overfitting;
- suppressed base rates;
- hidden assumption chains;
- elegant but weakly supported stories.
## 9. Synthesis
The final report must be useful for decisions.
That means:
- direct answer first;
- confidence not buried;
- uncertainty surfaced;
- key evidence clearly presented;
- alternative explanations not omitted;
- actionable signposts included.
## 10. Completion discipline
Do not confuse “I found something interesting” with “the task is complete”.
Before exit:
- every sub-question has a state;
- major contradictions are acknowledged;
- open gaps are listed;
- confidence matches the evidence base;
- the answer addresses the user’s actual question.
# Quick operator checklist
Use this short checklist during synthesis:
- Did I answer the actual question?
- Did I separate fact from inference?
- Did I include the strongest alternative explanation?
- Did I look for contradiction, not only support?
- Did I downgrade confidence where evidence is thin?
- Did I show what would change the conclusion?
