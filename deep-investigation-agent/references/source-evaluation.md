# Source Evaluation
This document defines how to judge source quality and how to avoid trust flattening.
## Core rule
A source is not “good” because it sounds confident, is popular, or aligns with the current narrative.
A source is stronger when it is:
- closer to the event,
- more transparent about method,
- less dependent on hidden intermediaries,
- more independently corroborated,
- less burdened by incentives or conflicts.
## Tier model
### Tier 1 — primary or near-primary evidence
Examples:
- laws, regulations, court filings;
- company filings and investor disclosures;
- trial registries and official health guidance;
- direct transcripts and official statements;
- raw data releases;
- satellite or geospatial evidence with provenance;
- original media with contextual verification;
- procurement and customs records.
Use Tier 1 wherever possible.
### Tier 2 — strong secondary synthesis
Examples:
- reputable wire services;
- high-quality investigative outlets;
- specialist research institutes;
- domain experts showing transparent methods;
- strong technical writeups with traceable references.
Tier 2 is useful when it clearly points back to source evidence.
### Tier 3 — weak or agenda-heavy sources
Examples:
- anonymous aggregators;
- viral threads without evidence;
- partisan amplification;
- recycled summaries;
- screenshots without provenance;
- “insider” claims with no verifiable basis.
Tier 3 can generate leads but cannot close an important question.
## Source scoring dimensions
Assess important sources across these dimensions:
- proximity to event;
- transparency of method;
- independence;
- domain expertise;
- conflict of interest;
- update history;
- corroboration;
- evidentiary specificity.
A source with weak ideology but strong records may still be useful.
A source with good branding but poor traceability may still be weak.
## Corroboration
Corroboration means independent confirmation.
It does not mean:
- ten articles copying one upstream report;
- multiple social accounts repeating the same screenshot;
- one claim echoed across commentary.
Look for:
- separate origin paths;
- independent evidence classes;
- different institutional incentives converging on the same fact.
## Quote verification
Never rely on clipped quotes if the full context can be checked.
Check:
- full transcript;
- original video or statement;
- time and date;
- surrounding sentence or paragraph;
- whether translation altered meaning.
## Numerical claim verification
For any important number:
- locate the original dataset or filing;
- identify units and definitions;
- check date range;
- verify whether nominal vs real, annualized vs absolute, cumulative vs period flow, per capita vs aggregate distinctions matter;
- note revisions or methodological caveats.
## Visual evidence checklist
When images or video matter, check:
- earliest discoverable upload;
- metadata if available;
- landmarks;
- weather or lighting consistency;
- language, signage, uniforms, vehicle markings;
- whether media has appeared earlier in another context;
- whether cropping removes key context.
## Health and science evidence
Default hierarchy:
- systematic reviews and strong guidelines;
- well-conducted randomized or comparative studies when relevant;
- registries and official surveillance;
- single papers with transparent methods;
- preprints clearly labeled as provisional;
- expert commentary;
- anecdotes last.
Always care about:
- methodology,
- sample size,
- effect size,
- uncertainty,
- generalizability,
- reproducibility.
## Red flags
Treat these as warning signs:
- dramatic conclusions with vague sourcing;
- confidence without methods;
- “experts say” with no names or documents;
- selective chronology;
- emotionally charged framing replacing evidence;
- heavy reliance on unnamed insiders;
- no distinction between claim and proof.
