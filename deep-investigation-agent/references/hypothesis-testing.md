# Hypothesis Testing
This document explains how to generate and test competing explanations.
## Why hypotheses matter
Analytical failure often begins when the first plausible explanation becomes the default lens for all later evidence.
Competing hypotheses force the agent to:
- test alternatives;
- make assumptions explicit;
- resist narrative lock-in;
- discover what evidence actually discriminates between explanations.
## Minimum hypothesis set
Unless the task is trivial, generate at least three hypotheses:
- H1 — obvious or mainstream explanation
- H2 — strongest alternative explanation
- H3 — mixed, structural, or indirect explanation
For highly ambiguous tasks, expand to four or five.
## Hypothesis template
For each hypothesis, record:
- title
- concise statement
- what it explains well
- what it struggles to explain
- required assumptions
- strongest supporting evidence
- strongest contradicting evidence
- expected indicators if true
- current confidence
## Evidence discrimination
Not all evidence separates hypotheses equally.
Useful discriminators:
- records that only make sense under one explanation;
- timeline elements that contradict one hypothesis;
- incentive structures that fit one explanation better;
- capability constraints;
- documented coordination vs mere parallel behavior;
- direct admissions or formal records.
Weak discriminators:
- mood,
- aesthetics,
- vibes,
- repeated insinuation,
- ideological convenience.
## Working matrix
Use a matrix mentally or in notes:
- rows = evidence items
- columns = hypotheses
- cell = supports / contradicts / neutral / ambiguous
This prevents subconscious weighting from drifting toward the prettiest story.
## Structural explanations
Do not neglect structural or mixed hypotheses.
Many real-world outcomes come from:
- institutional incentives,
- market structure,
- bureaucratic inertia,
- regulatory lag,
- fragmented coordination,
- local optimization producing system-wide effects.
These are often less dramatic but more realistic than hidden master-plan stories.
## Hidden coordination and covert arrangements
Investigating non-obvious coordination is valid.
But distinguish:
- documented coordination,
- probable coordination,
- pattern-based suspicion,
- speculation.
You may escalate confidence only when the evidence justifies it.
Examples of stronger signals:
- overlapping advisers plus synchronized timing plus documented funding plus direct communications;
- formal records, court documents, leaked authentic materials, or on-record admissions.
Examples of weak signals:
- shared ideology alone;
- co-attendance at public events;
- vague personal proximity;
- repeated rhetorical similarity without any hard connective tissue.
## Forecast-oriented testing
Hypotheses should imply future observables.
Ask:
- If H1 is true, what happens next?
- If H2 is true, what signs should emerge?
- What evidence would increase or reduce confidence?
This makes analysis adaptive rather than static.
## Confidence discipline
Confidence is about basis quality, not emotional certainty.
Raise confidence when:
- evidence is direct,
- sources are independent,
- assumptions are light,
- contradictions are limited.
Lower confidence when:
- evidence is indirect,
- alternative explanations remain strong,
- contradictions are unresolved,
- the story relies on stacked assumptions.
